The Stars

A star is a luminous sphere of plasma that's held together by gravity so strong, fusion occurs. In the universe, there are many different types of stars. They each form in different ways and have different appearances. They also have distinct properties and behaviors. If one is going to study astronomy, the different types of stars are a must to learn about.

  • Protostars
  • This is a protostar, which is a star is the earliest phase of stellar evolution
  • It forms when a molecular cloud undergos self-gravity, causing a core to emerge
  • The more mass the star has, the faster it will undergo change
  • This phase may take anywhere from 100,000 years to 100 million
  • Some examples of protostars are T Tauri, HL Tauri, and W33A
  • Brown Dwarfs
  • This is a brown dwarf, a possible result of a protostar
  • Brown dwarfs are "failed stars" as they can't fuse hydrogen into helium
  • Their size ranges from 15 times the mass of Jupiter to 75 times
  • They can take about 10 million years to cool down after formation
  • Some examples of brown dwarfs are Gliese 229B and 54 Piscium
  • Red Dwarfs
  • This is a red dwarf, a possible outcome of a protostar
  • Red dwarfs are the most common type of stars
  • Their sizes can range from .08 to .6 times that of the Sun
  • They can take tens of billions or trillions of years to cool off
  • Some examples of red dwarfs are Proxima Centauri and Barnard's Star
  • Yellow Dwarfs
  • This is a yellow dwarf, a possible result of a protostar
  • Their color ranges from white to light yellow
  • Their sizes can range from .84 to 1.14 times that of out Sun
  • They can burn for 10 billion years before turning into a red giant
  • Eaxmples of yellow dwarfs are our Sun and Alpha Centauri
  • Red Giant
  • This is a red giant, the aftermath of a yellow dwarf
  • The hydrogen is exhausted at the core, causing it to expand to its current size
  • Red giants can last from a few thousand to a billion years
  • They can become anywhere from 100 to 1,000 times as big as our Sun
  • Examples of red giants are Aldebaran, Arcturus, and Gacrux
  • White Dwarfs
  • This is a white dwarf, the result of a red dwarf and red giant
  • Once a red dwarf cools or a red giant sheds its layers, a white dwarf is left
  • They can take tens or hundreds of billions of years to cool
  • They can be slighty bigger than Earth but weigh as much as the Sun
  • Examples of white dwarfs are Sirius B and Procyon B
  • Neutron Stars
  • This is a neutron star, the result of a supernova
  • When gravity collapses, protons and electrons merge into neutrons
  • Over time, the star will cool, eventually leaving a cold dead star
  • Their radius can be 10 kilometers, but a spoonful can weigh 4 billion tons
